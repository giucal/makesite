Ontology

First draft: 2021-02-17

This essay summarizes my metaphysical wanderings since I’ve been studying Hume, Kant, and the idealists in high school. I hope you’ll enjoy reading about them as much as I enjoyed living through them.

The concept of substance is unnecessary for explaining the fact that we experience reality; form suffices.

Uncritically assuming this thesis for a moment, we can derive that:

  1. All worlds, per se, have the same ontological dignity.
  2. This world, reality, is special only in the sense we are part of it.
  3. The fact that we have an experience is a structural feature of this world. The fact that we can say that it exists is too a structural feature of this world.
  4. The same can be said of any subject of any world: all the worlds are present to their subjects, if they have some; but these worlds are—to steal an effective metaphor—without windows.

Now, for the boring part.

Premises

Existence

When we say that something exists, we often mean that it’s part of reality. Often, not always; but insofar as metaphysics is concerned, existence as being there always implies a reality. The Moon exists because it’s there in the sky. Children will say that the Tooth Fairy exists because, after all, someone must be collecting their teeth in exchange for cents; to them, she is and acts in the world. Believers will say that God exists, by which they mean He’s out there.

This is, I believe, the conventional interpretation of existence: the fact that something is not just in our mind, but is part of a broader reality, regardless of what reality actually is. It may not be the only interpretation, but I guess it entails much of the relevant usage. I say regardless of what reality actually is for, when we turn to establish what reality means, we find ourselves referring one way or another back to existence.

Is reality everything? It can’t be, since everything is too broad a term: it includes concepts, thoughts, works of imagination, mathematical systems, etc., things that we might not be willing to call real.

Is reality the sum of everything which… exists? That’s a good characterization of reality, despite being tautological. Had we chosen a definition of existence that didn’t refer to reality, we might have arrived at different conclusions. But a different definition of existence would still need to be consistent with common use; otherwise we’d be talking past each other, as it’s often the case with philosophy, especially metaphysics. Having found no good alternative, I’ll leave definitions as they are; but in doing so, I encourage you to ponder on the prominent or immaterial role they play in establishing the validity of the thesis.

The relationship between the two concepts of existence and reality is firm yet purely formal. Existence is the property of what belongs to reality, which in turn is the class of all that exists. But such relationship only tells us of the existence of things which we already know whether are part of reality; and vice versa, it only tells us whether something is part of reality which we already know whether exists. Given a thing x, we can say whether x exists only if we already know whether it is part of reality; and vice versa. Thus, existence and reality are primitive concepts—like points and segments in geometry—whose relative meaning—the meaning they carry between themselves—is conventional and can be explained, but whose absolute meaning—that they carry with respect to our experience—despite also conventional, can only be shown [Wittgenstein]. Indeed, we learn the meaning of primitive concepts by examples.
